The California Privacy Rights Act of 2020

Proposition 24 in the November 2020 General Election

[**SECTION 1. Title.**](https://thecpra.org/#SECTION%201)

This measure shall be known and may be cited as “The California Privacy Rights Act of 2020.”

[**SEC. 2. Findings and Declarations.**](https://thecpra.org/#SEC.%202)

The People of the State of California hereby find and declare all of the following:

[**(A)**](https://thecpra.org/#SEC.%202(A)) In 1972, California voters amended the California Constitution to include the right of privacy among the “inalienable” rights of all people. Voters acted in response to the accelerating encroachment on personal freedom and security caused by increased data [collection](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(f)) and usage in contemporary society. The amendment established a legal and enforceable constitutional right of privacy for every Californian. Fundamental to this right of privacy is the ability of individuals to [control](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d)(2)) the use, including the [sale](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(ad)), of their [personal information](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(v)).

[**(B)**](https://thecpra.org/#SEC.%202(B)) Since California voters approved the constitutional right of privacy, the California Legislature has adopted specific mechanisms to safeguard Californians’ privacy, including the Online Privacy Protection Act, the Privacy Rights for California Minors in the Digital World Act, and Shine the Light, but [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i))s had no right to learn what [personal information](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(v)) a [business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d)) had [collected](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(f)) about them and how they used it or to direct [business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d))es not to [sell](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(ad)) the [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i))’s [personal information](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(v)).

[**(C)**](https://thecpra.org/#SEC.%202(C)) That changed in 2018, when more than 629,000 California voters signed petitions to qualify the California [Consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i)) Privacy Act of 2018 for the ballot. In response to the measure’s qualification, the Legislature enacted the California [Consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i)) Privacy Act of 2018 (CCPA) into law. The CCPA gives California [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i))s the right to learn what information a [business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d)) has [collected](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(f)) about them, to delete their [personal information](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(v)), to stop [business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d))es from [selling](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(ad)) their [personal information](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(v)), including using it to target them with ads that follow them as they browse the internet from one website to another, and to hold [business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d))es accountable if they do not take reasonable steps to safeguard their [personal information](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(v)).

[**(D)**](https://thecpra.org/#SEC.%202(D)) Even before the CCPA had gone into effect, the Legislature considered many bills in 2019 to amend the law, some of which would have significantly weakened it. Unless California voters take action, the hard‐fought rights [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i))s have won could be undermined by future legislation.

[**(E)**](https://thecpra.org/#SEC.%202(E)) Rather than diluting privacy rights, California should strengthen them over time. Many [business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d))es [collect](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(f)) and use [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i))s’ [personal information](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(v)), sometimes without [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i))s’ knowledge regarding the [business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d))’s use and retention of their [personal information](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(v)). In practice, [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i))s are often entering into a form of contractual arrangement in which while they do not pay money for a good or [service](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(af)), they exchange access to that good or [service](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(af)) in return for access to their attention, or access to their [personal information](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(v)). Because the value of the [personal information](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(v)) they are exchanging for the good or [service](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(af)) is often opaque, depending on the practices of the [business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d)), [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i))s often have no good way to value the transaction. In addition, the terms of agreement or policies in which the arrangements are spelled out, are often complex, unclear, and as a result most [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i))s never have the time to read or understand them.

[**(F)**](https://thecpra.org/#SEC.%202(F)) This asymmetry of information makes it difficult for [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i))s to understand what they are exchanging and therefore to negotiate effectively with [business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d))es. Unlike in other areas of the economy where [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i))s can comparison shop, or can understand at a glance if a good or [service](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(af)) is expensive or affordable, it is hard for the [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i)) to know how much his or her information is worth to any given [business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d)), when data use practices vary so widely between [business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d))es.

[**(G)**](https://thecpra.org/#SEC.%202(G)) The State therefore has an interest in mandating laws that will allow [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i))s to understand more fully how their information is being used, and for what purposes. In the same way that ingredient labels on foods help [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i))s shop more effectively, disclosure around data management practices will help [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i))s become more informed counterparties in the data economy, and promote competition. Additionally, if a [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i)) can tell a [business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d)) not to [sell](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(ad)) his or her data, then that [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i)) will not have to scour a privacy policy to see whether the [business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d)) is, in fact, [selling](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(ad)) that data, and the resulting savings in time is worth, in the aggregate, a tremendous amount of money.

[**(H)**](https://thecpra.org/#SEC.%202(H))[Consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i))s need stronger laws to place them on a more equal footing when negotiating with [business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d))es in order to protect their rights. [Consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i))s should be entitled to a clear explanation of the uses of their [personal information](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(v)), including how it is used for advertising, and to [control](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d)(2)), correct, or delete it, including by allowing [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i))s to limit [business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d))es’ use of their [sensitive](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(ae))[personal information](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(v)) to help guard against identity theft, to opt‐out of the [sale](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(ad)) and sharing of their [personal information](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(v)), and to request that [business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d))es correct inaccurate information about them.

[**(I)**](https://thecpra.org/#SEC.%202(I)) California is the world leader in many new technologies that have reshaped our society. The world today is unimaginable without the internet, one of the most momentous inventions in human history, and the new [services](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(af)) and [business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d))es that arose on top of it ‐‐ many of which were invented here in California. One of the most successful [business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d)) models for the internet has been [services](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(af)) that rely on advertising to make money as opposed to charging [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i))s a fee. Advertising‐supported [services](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(af)) have existed for generations, and can be a great model for [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i))s and [business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d))es alike. However, some advertising [business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d))es today use technologies and tools that are opaque to [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i))s to [collect](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(f)) and trade vast amounts of [personal information](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(v)), to track them across the internet, and to create detailed profiles of their individual interests. Some companies that do not charge [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i))s a fee, subsidize these [services](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(af)) by monetizing [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i))s’ [personal information](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(v)). [Consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i))s should have the information and tools necessary to limit the use of their information to non‐invasive, pro‐privacy advertising, where their [personal information](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(v)) is not [sold](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(ad)) to or shared with hundreds of [business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d))es they’ve never heard of, if they choose to do so. Absent these tools, it will be virtually impossible for [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i))s to fully understand these contracts they are essentially entering into when they interact with various [business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d))es.

[**(J)**](https://thecpra.org/#SEC.%202(J)) Children are particularly vulnerable from a negotiating perspective with respect to their privacy rights. Parents should be able to [control](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d)(2)) what information is [collected](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(f)) and [sold](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(ad)) or shared about their young children and should be given the right to demand that companies erase information [collected](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(f)) about their children.

[**(K)**](https://thecpra.org/#SEC.%202(K))[Business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d)) should also be held directly accountable to [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i))s for data security breaches and notify [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i))s when their most sensitive information has been compromised.

[**(L)**](https://thecpra.org/#SEC.%202(L)) An independent watchdog whose mission is to protect [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i)) privacy should ensure that [business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d))es and [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i))s are well‐informed about their rights and obligations and should vigorously enforce the law against [business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d))es that violate [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i))s’ privacy rights.

[**SEC. 3. Purpose and Intent.**](https://thecpra.org/#SEC.%203)

In enacting this Act, it is the purpose and intent of the people of the State of California to further protect [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i))s’ rights, including the constitutional right of privacy. The implementation of this Act shall be guided by the following principles:

[**(A)**](https://thecpra.org/#SEC.%203(A))[Consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i)) Rights

[**(1)**](https://thecpra.org/#SEC.%203(A)(1))[Consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i))s should know who is [collect](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(f))ing their [personal information](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(v)) and that of their children, how it is being used, and to whom it is disclosed, so that they have the information necessary to exercise meaningful [control](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d)(2)) over [business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d))es’ use of their [personal information](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(v)) and that of their children.

[**(2)**](https://thecpra.org/#SEC.%203(A)(2))[Consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i))s should be able to [control](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d)(2)) the use of their [personal information](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(v)), including limiting the use of their [sensitive](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(ae))[personal information](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(v)), the unauthorized use or disclosure of which creates a heightened risk of harm to the [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i)), and they should have meaningful options over how it is [collected](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(f)), used, and disclosed.

[**(3)**](https://thecpra.org/#SEC.%203(A)(3))[Consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i))s should have access to their [personal information](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(v)) and should be able to correct it, delete it, and take it with them from one [business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d)) to another.

[**(4)**](https://thecpra.org/#SEC.%203(A)(4))[Consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i))s or their authorized agents should be able to exercise these options through easily accessible self‐serve tools.

[**(5)**](https://thecpra.org/#SEC.%203(A)(5))[Consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i))s should be able to exercise these rights without being penalized for doing so.

[**(6)**](https://thecpra.org/#SEC.%203(A)(6))[Consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i))s should be able to hold [business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d))es accountable for failing to take reasonable precautions to protect their most [sensitive](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(ae))[personal information](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(v)) from hackers and security breaches.

[**(7)**](https://thecpra.org/#SEC.%203(A)(7))[Consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i))s should benefit from [business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d))es’ use of their [personal information](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(v)).

[**(8)**](https://thecpra.org/#SEC.%203(A)(8)) The privacy interests of employees and independent contractors should also be protected, taking into account the differences in the relationship between employees or independent contractors and [business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d))es, as compared to the relationship between [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i))s and [business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d))es. In addition, this law is not intended to interfere with the right to organize and [collect](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(f))ive bargaining under the National Labor Relations Act. It is the purpose and intent of the Act to extend the exemptions in this title for employee and [business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d)) to [business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d)) communications until January 1, 2023.

[**(B)**](https://thecpra.org/#SEC.%203(B)) The Responsibilities of [Business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d))es

[**(1)**](https://thecpra.org/#SEC.%203(B)(1))[Business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d))es should specifically and clearly inform [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i))s about how they [collect](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(f)) and use [personal information](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(v)) and how they can exercise their rights and choice.

[**(2)**](https://thecpra.org/#SEC.%203(B)(2))[Business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d))es should only [collect](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(f))[consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i))s’ [personal information](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(v)) for specific, explicit, and legitimate disclosed purposes, and should not further [collect](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(f)), use, or disclose [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i))s’ [personal information](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(v)) for reasons incompatible with those purposes.

[**(3)**](https://thecpra.org/#SEC.%203(B)(3))[Business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d))es should [collect](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(f))[consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i))s’ [personal information](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(v)) only to the extent that it is relevant and limited to what is necessary in relation to the purposes for which it is being [collected](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(f)), used, and shared.

[**(4)**](https://thecpra.org/#SEC.%203(B)(4))[Business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d))es should provide [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i))s or their authorized agents with easily accessible means to allow [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i))s and their children to obtain their [personal information](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(v)), to delete it, or correct it, and to opt‐out of its [sale](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(ad)) and the sharing across [business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d)) platforms, [services](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(af)), [business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d))es and [device](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(o))s, and to limit the use of their [sensitive](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(ae))[personal information](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(v)).

[**(5)**](https://thecpra.org/#SEC.%203(B)(5))[Business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d))es should not penalize [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i))s for exercising these rights.

[**(6)**](https://thecpra.org/#SEC.%203(B)(6))[Business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d))es should take reasonable precautions to protect [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i))s’ [personal information](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(v)) from a security breach.

[**(7)**](https://thecpra.org/#SEC.%203(B)(7))[Business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d))es should be held accountable when they violate [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i))s’ privacy rights, and the penalties should be higher when the violation affects children.

[**(C)**](https://thecpra.org/#SEC.%203(C)) Implementation of the Law

[**(1)**](https://thecpra.org/#SEC.%203(C)(1)) The rights of [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i))s and the responsibilities of [business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d))es should be implemented with the goal of strengthening [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i)) privacy, while giving attention to the impact on [business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d)) and innovation. [Consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i)) privacy and the development of beneficial new products and [services](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(af)) are not necessarily incompatible goals. Strong [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i)) privacy rights create incentives to innovate and develop new products that are privacy protective.

[**(2)**](https://thecpra.org/#SEC.%203(C)(2))[Business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d))es and [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i))s should be provided with clear guidance about their responsibilities and rights.

[**(3)**](https://thecpra.org/#SEC.%203(C)(3)) The law should place the [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i)) in a position to knowingly and freely negotiate with a [business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d)) over the [business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d))’ use of the [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i))’s [personal information](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(v)).

[**(4)**](https://thecpra.org/#SEC.%203(C)(4)) The law should adjust to technological changes, help [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i))s exercise their rights, and assist [business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d))es with compliance, with the continuing goal of strengthening [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i)) privacy.

[**(5)**](https://thecpra.org/#SEC.%203(C)(5)) The law should enable pro‐[consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i)) new products and [services](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(af)) and promote efficiency of implementation for [business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d)), provided that the amendments do not compromise or weaken [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i)) privacy.

[**(6)**](https://thecpra.org/#SEC.%203(C)(6)) The law should be amended, if necessary, to improve its operation, provided that the amendments do not compromise or weaken [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i)) privacy, while giving attention to the impact on [business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d)) and innovation.

[**(7)**](https://thecpra.org/#SEC.%203(C)(7))[Business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d))es should be held accountable for violating the law through vigorous administrative and civil enforcement.

[**(8)**](https://thecpra.org/#SEC.%203(C)(8)) To the extent it advances [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i)) privacy and [business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d)) compliance, the law should be compatible with privacy laws in other jurisdictions.

[**SEC. 4. Section 1798.100 of the Civil Code is amended to read:**](https://thecpra.org/#SEC.%204)

[**1798.100. General Duties of Businesses that Collect Personal Information**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.100)

[**(a)**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.100(a)) A [business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d)) that [control](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d)(2))s the [collection](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(f)) of a [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i))’s [personal information](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(v)) shall, at or before the point of [collection](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(f)), inform [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i))s as to:

[**(1)**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.100(a)(1)) the categories of [personal information](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(v)) to be [collected](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(f)) and the purposes for which the categories of [personal information](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(v)) are [collected](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(f)) or used and whether such information is [sold](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(ad)) or shared. A [business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d)) shall not [collect](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(f)) additional categories of [personal information](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(v)) or use [personal information](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(v))[collected](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(f)) for additional purposes that are incompatible with the disclosed purpose for which the [personal information](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(v)) was [collected](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(f)), without providing the [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i)) with notice consistent with this section.

[**(2)**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.100(a)(2)) if the [business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d))[collects](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(f))[sensitive](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(ae))[personal information](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(v)), the categories of [sensitive](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(ae))[personal information](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(v)) to be [collected](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(f)) and the purposes for which the categories of [sensitive](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(ae))[personal information](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(v)) are [collected](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(f)) or used and whether such information is [sold](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(ad)) or shared. A [business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d)) shall not [collect](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(f)) additional categories of [sensitive](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(ae))[personal information](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(v)) or use [sensitive](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(ae))[personal information](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(v))[collected](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(f)) for additional purposes that are incompatible with the disclosed purpose for which the [sensitive](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(ae))[personal information](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(v)) was [collected](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(f)), without providing the [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i)) with notice consistent with this section.

[**(3)**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.100(a)(3)) the length of time the [business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d)) intends to retain each category of [personal information](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(v)), including [sensitive](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(ae))[personal information](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(v)), or if that is not possible, the criteria used to determine such period, provided that a [business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d)) shall not retain a [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i))’s [personal information](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(v)) or [sensitive](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(ae))[personal information](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(v)) for each disclosed purpose for which the [personal information](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(v)) was [collected](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(f)) for longer than is reasonably necessary for that disclosed purpose.

[**(b)**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.100(b)) A [business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d)) that, acting as a [third party](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(ai)), [control](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d)(2))s the [collection](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(f)) of [personal information](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(v)) about a [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i)) may satisfy its obligation under subdivision (a) by providing the required information prominently and conspicuously on the [homepage](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(p)) of its internet website. [**(a)**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(a)) “[Advertising and marketing](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(a))” means a communication by a [business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d)) or a [person](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(u))acting on the [business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d))’s behalf in any medium intended to induce a [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i)) to obtain goods, [services](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(af)), or employment.

[**(b)**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(b)) “[Aggregate](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(b))[consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i)) information” means information that relates to a group or category of [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i))s, from which individual [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i)) identities have been removed, that is not linked or reasonably linkable to any [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i)) or [household](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(q)), including via a [device](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(o)). “[Aggregate](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(b))[consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i)) information” does not mean one or more individual [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i)) records that have been [deidentified](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(m)).

[**(c)**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(c)) “[Biometric information](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(c))” means an individual’s physiological, biological or behavioral characteristics, including information pertaining to an individual’s deoxyribonucleic acid (DNA), that is used or intended to be used, singly or in combination with each other or with other identifying data, to establish individual identity. [Biometric information](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(c)) includes, but is not limited to, imagery of the iris, retina, fingerprint, face, hand, palm, vein patterns, and voice recordings, from which an identifier template, such as a faceprint, a minutiae template, or a voiceprint, can be extracted, and keystroke patterns or rhythms, gait patterns or rhythms, and sleep, health, or exercise data that contain identifying information.

[**(2)**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.145(a)(2)) Comply with a civil, criminal, or regulatory inquiry, investigation, subpoena, or summons by federal, state, or local authorities. Law enforcement agencies, including police and sheriff’s departments, may direct a [business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d)) pursuant to a law enforcement agency‐approved investigation with an active case number not to delete a [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i))’s [personal information](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(v)) and upon receipt of such direction a [business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d)) shall not delete the [personal information](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(v)) for 90 days, in order to allow the law enforcement agency to obtain a court‐issued subpoena, order, or warrant to obtain a [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i))’s [personal information](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(v)). [**(ii)**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.185(a)(20)(C)(ii)) does not require or imply that the [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i)) must click the link to receive full functionality of any products or [services](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(af)), including the website;

[**(iii)**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.185(a)(20)(C)(iii)) does not make use of any [dark pattern](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(l))s; and

[**(iv)**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.185(a)(20)(C)(iv)) applies only to the [business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d)) with which the [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i)) intends to interact. The regulation should strive to curb coercive or deceptive practices in response to an opt‐out preference signal but should not unduly restrict [business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d))es that are trying in good faith to comply with Section [1798.135](https://thecpra.org/#1798.135).

[**(21)**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.185(a)(21)) Review existing California Insurance Code provisions and regulations relating to [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i)) privacy, except those relating to insurance rates or pricing, to determine whether any provisions of the Insurance Code provide greater protection to [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i))s than the provisions of this Title. Upon completing its review, the Agency shall adopt a regulation that applies only the more protective provisions of this Title to insurance companies. For the purpose of clarity, the Insurance Commissioner shall have jurisdiction over insurance rates and pricing.

[**(22)**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.185(a)(22)) Harmonizing the regulations governing opt‐out mechanisms, notices to [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i))s, and other operational mechanisms in this title to promote clarity and the functionality of this title for [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i))s.

[**(b)**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.185(b)) The Attorney General may adopt additional regulations as necessary to further the purposes of this title.

[**(c)**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.185(c)) The Attorney General shall not bring an enforcement action under this title until six months after the publication of the final regulations issued pursuant to this section or July 1, 2020, whichever is sooner.

[**(d)**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.185(d)) Notwithstanding subdivision (a), the timeline for adopting final regulations required by the Act adding this subdivision shall be July 1, 2022. Beginning the later of July 1, 2021, or six months after the Agency provides notice to the Attorney General that it is prepared to begin rulemaking under this title, the authority assigned to the Attorney General to adopt regulations under this section shall be exercised by the California Privacy Protection Agency. Notwithstanding any other law, civil and administrative enforcement of the provisions of law added or amended by this Act shall not commence until July 1, 2023, and shall only apply to violations occurring on or after that date. Enforcement of provisions of law contained in the California [Consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i)) Privacy Act of 2018 amended or reenacted by this Act shall remain in effect and shall be enforceable until the same provisions of this Act become enforceable.

[**SEC. 22. Section 1798.190 of the Civil Code is amended to read:**](https://thecpra.org/#SEC.%2022)

[**1798.190. Anti‐Avoidance**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.190)

A court or the Agency shall disregard the intermediate steps or transactions for purposes of effectuating the purposes of this title:

[**(a)**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.190(a)) if a series of steps or transactions were component parts of a single transaction intended from the beginning to be taken with the intention of avoiding the reach of this title, including the disclosure of information by a [business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d)) to a [third party](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(ai)) in order to avoid the definition of [sell](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(ad)) or share; or

[**(b)**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.190(b)) if steps or transactions were taken to purposely avoid the definition of [sell](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(ad)) or share by eliminating any monetary or other valuable consideration, including by entering into contracts that do not include an exchange for monetary or other valuable consideration, but where a party is obtaining something of value or use.

[**SEC. 23. Section 1798.192 of the Civil Code is amended to read:**](https://thecpra.org/#SEC.%2023)

[**1798.192. Waiver**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.192)

Any provision of a contract or agreement of any kind, including a representative action waiver, that purports to waive or limit in any way rights under this title, including, but not limited to, any right to a remedy or means of enforcement, shall be deemed contrary to public policy and shall be void and unenforceable. This section shall not prevent a [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i)) from declining to request information from a [business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d)), declining to opt‐out of a [business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d))’s [sale](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(ad)) of the [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i))’s [personal information](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(v)), or authorizing a [business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d)) to [sell](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(ad)) or share the [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i))’s [personal information](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(v)) after previously opting‐out.

[**SEC. 24. Section 1798.199.10 et seq. are added to the Civil Code to read as follows:**](https://thecpra.org/#SEC.%2024)

Establishment of California Privacy Protection Agency

[**1798.199.10.**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.199.10)

[**(a)**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.199.10(a)) There is hereby established in state government the California Privacy Protection Agency, which is vested with full administrative power, authority, and jurisdiction to implement and enforce the California [Consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i)) Privacy Act. The Agency shall be governed by a five‐member board, including the Chair. The Chair and one member of the board shall be appointed by the Governor. The Attorney General, Senate Rules Committee, and Speaker of the Assembly shall each appoint one member. These appointments should be made from among Californians with expertise in the areas of privacy, technology, and [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i)) rights.

[**(b)**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.199.10(b)) The initial appointments to the Agency shall be made within 90 days of the effective date of the Act adding this section.

[**1798.199.15.**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.199.15)

Members of the Agency board shall:

[**(a)**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.199.15(a)) have qualifications, experience and skills, in particular in the areas of privacy and technology, required to perform the duties of the Agency and exercise its powers;

[**(b)**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.199.15(b)) maintain the confidentiality of information which has come to their knowledge in the course of the performance of their tasks or exercise of their powers, except to the extent that disclosure is required by the Public Records Act;

[**(c)**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.199.15(c)) remain free from external influence, whether direct or indirect, and shall neither seek nor take instructions from another;

[**(d)**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.199.15(d)) refrain from any action incompatible with their duties and engaging in any incompatible occupation, whether gainful or not, during their term;

[**(e)**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.199.15(e)) have the right of access to all information made available by the Agency to the Chair;

[**(f)**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.199.15(f)) be precluded, for a period of one year after leaving office, from accepting employment with a [business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d)) that was subject to an enforcement action or civil action under this Title during the member’s tenure or during the five‐year period preceding the member’s appointment; and

[**(g)**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.199.15(g)) be precluded for a period of two years after leaving office, from acting, for compensation, as an agent or attorney for, or otherwise representing any other [person](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(u))in a matter pending before the Agency if the purpose is to influence an action of the Agency.

[**1798.199.20.**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.199.20)

Members of the Agency board, including the Chair, shall serve at the pleasure of their appointing authority but shall serve for no longer than eight consecutive years.

[**1798.199.25.**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.199.25)

For each day on which they engage in official duties, members of the Agency board shall be compensated at the rate of one hundred dollars ($100), adjusted biennially to reflect changes in the cost of living, and shall be reimbursed for expenses incurred in performance of their official duties.

[**1798.199.30.**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.199.30)

The Agency board shall appoint an executive director who shall act in accordance with Agency policies and regulations and with applicable law. The Agency shall appoint and discharge officers, counsel, and employees, consistent with applicable civil [service](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(af)) laws, and shall fix the compensation of employees and prescribe their duties. The Agency may contract for [services](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(af)) that cannot be provided by its employees.

[**1798.199.35.**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.199.35)

The Agency board may delegate authority to the Chair or the executive director to act in the name of the Agency between meetings of the Agency, except with respect to resolution of enforcement actions and rulemaking authority.

[**1798.199.40.**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.199.40)

The Agency shall perform the following functions:

[**(a)**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.199.40(a)) Administer, implement, and enforce through administrative actions, Title 1.81.5 (commencing with Section [1798.100](https://thecpra.org/#1798.100)) to Part 4 of Division 3 of the Civil Code.

[**(b)**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.199.40(b)) On and after the earlier of July 1, 2021, or within six months of the Agency providing the Attorney General with notice that it is prepared to assume rulemaking responsibilities under this title, adopt, amend, and rescind regulations pursuant to Section [1798.185](https://thecpra.org/#1798.185) to carry out the purposes and provisions of the California [Consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i)) Privacy Act, including regulations specifying record keeping requirements for [business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d))es to ensure compliance with this title.

[**(c)**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.199.40(c)) Through the implementation of this title, protect the fundamental privacy rights of natural [persons](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(u)) with respect to the use of their [personal information](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(v)).

[**(d)**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.199.40(d)) Promote public awareness and understanding of the risks, rules, responsibilities, safeguards, and rights in relation to the [collection](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(f)), use, [sale](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(ad)) and disclosure of [personal information](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(v)), including the rights of minors with respect to their own information, and provide a public report summarizing the risk assessments filed with the Agency pursuant to paragraph (15) of subdivision (a) of Section [1798.185](https://thecpra.org/#1798.185) while ensuring that data security is not compromised.

[**(e)**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.199.40(e)) Provide guidance to [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i))s regarding their rights under this title.

[**(f)**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.199.40(f)) Provide guidance to [business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d))es regarding their duties and responsibilities under this title, and appoint a Chief Privacy Auditor to conduct audits of [business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d))es to ensure compliance with this title pursuant to regulations adopted pursuant to paragraph (18) of subdivision (a) of Section [1798.185](https://thecpra.org/#1798.185).

[**(g)**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.199.40(g)) Provide technical assistance and advice to the Legislature, upon request, with respect to privacy‐related legislation.

[**(h)**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.199.40(h)) Monitor relevant developments relating to the protection of [personal information](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(v)), and in particular, the development of information and communication technologies and commercial practices.

[**(i)**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.199.40(i)) Cooperate with other agencies with jurisdiction over privacy laws and with data [processing](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(y)) authorities in California, other states, territories, and countries to ensure consistent application of privacy protections.

[**(j)**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.199.40(j)) Establish a mechanism pursuant to which [persons](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(u)) doing [business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d)) in California that do not meet the definition of [business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d)) set forth in paragraphs (1), (2), or (3) of subdivision (d) of section [1798.140](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140) may voluntarily certify that they are in compliance with this title, as set forth in paragraph (4) of subdivision (d) of Section [1798.140](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140), and make a list of such entities available to the public.

[**(k)**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.199.40(k)) Solicit, review, and approve applications for grants to the extent funds are available pursuant to paragraph (2) of subdivision (b) of Section [1798.160](https://thecpra.org/#1798.160).

[**(l)**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.199.40(l)) Perform all other acts necessary or appropriate in the exercise of its power, authority, and jurisdiction, and seek to balance the goals of strengthening [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i)) privacy while giving attention to the impact on [business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d))es.

[**1798.199.45.**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.199.45)

Upon the sworn complaint of any [person](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(u))or on its own initiative, the Agency may investigate possible violations of this title relating to any [business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d)), [service provider](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(ag)(1)), contractor, or person. The Agency may decide not to investigate a complaint or decide to provide a [business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d)) with a time‐period to cure the alleged violation. In making a decision not to investigate or provide more time to cure, the Agency may consider:

[**(a)**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.199.45(a)) the lack of intent to violate this title; and

[**(b)**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.199.45(b)) voluntary efforts undertaken by the [business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d)), [service provider](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(ag)(1)), contractor, or [person](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(u))to cure the alleged violation prior to being notified by the Agency of the complaint.

The Agency shall notify in writing the [person](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(u))who made the complaint of the action, if any, the Agency has taken or plans to take on the complaint, together with the reasons for such action or non‐action.

[**1798.199.50.**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.199.50)

No finding of probable cause to believe this title has been violated shall be made by the Agency unless, at least 30 days prior to the Agency’s consideration of the alleged violation, the [business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d)), [service provider](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(ag)(1)), contractor, or [person](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(u))alleged to have violated this title is notified of the violation by [service](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(af)) of process or registered mail with return receipt requested, provided with a summary of the evidence, and informed of their right to be present in [person](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(u))and represented by counsel at any proceeding of the Agency held for the purpose of considering whether probable cause exists for believing the [person](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(u))violated this title. Notice to the alleged violator shall be deemed made on the date of [service](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(af)), the date the registered mail receipt is signed, or if the registered mail receipt is not signed, the date returned by the post office. A proceeding held for the purpose of considering probable cause shall be private unless the alleged violator files with the Agency a written request that the proceeding be public.

[**1798.199.55.**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.199.55)

[**(a)**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.199.55(a)) When the Agency determines there is probable cause for believing this title has been violated, it shall hold a hearing to determine if a violation has or violations have occurred. Notice shall be given and the hearing conducted in accordance with the Administrative Procedure Act (Chapter 5 (commencing with Section 11500), Part 1, Division 3, Title 2, Government Code). The Agency shall have all the powers granted by that chapter. If the Agency determines on the basis of the hearing conducted pursuant to this subdivision that a violation or violations have occurred, it shall issue an order that may require the violator to do all or any of the following:

[**(1)**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.199.55(a)(1)) Cease and desist violation of this title.

[**(2)**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.199.55(a)(2)) Subject to Section [1798.155](https://thecpra.org/#1798.155), pay an administrative fine of up to two thousand five hundred dollars ($2,500) for each violation, or up to seven thousand five hundred dollars ($7,500) for each intentional violation and each violation involving the [personal information](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(v)) of minor [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i))s to the [Consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i)) Privacy Fund within the General Fund of the state. When the Agency determines that no violation has occurred, it shall publish a declaration so stating.

[**(b)**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.199.55(b)) If two or more [persons](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(u)) are responsible for any violation or violations, they shall be jointly and severally liable.

[**1798.199.60.**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.199.60)

Whenever the Agency rejects the decision of an administrative law judge made pursuant to Section 11517 of the Government Code, the Agency shall state the reasons in writing for rejecting the decision.

[**1798.199.65.**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.199.65)

The Agency may subpoena witnesses, compel their attendance and testimony, administer oaths and affirmations, take evidence and require by subpoena the production of any books, papers, records or other items material to the performance of the Agency’s duties or exercise of its powers, including but not limited to its power to audit a [business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d))’s compliance with this title.

[**1798.199.70.**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.199.70)

No administrative action brought pursuant to this title alleging a violation of any of the provisions of this title shall be commenced more than five years after the date on which the violation occurred.

[**(a)**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.199.70(a)) The [service](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(af)) of the probable cause hearing notice, as required by Section [1798.199.50](https://thecpra.org/#1798.199.50), upon the [person](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(u))alleged to have violated this title shall constitute the commencement of the administrative action.

[**(b)**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.199.70(b)) If the [person](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(u))alleged to have violated this title engages in the fraudulent concealment of his or her acts or identity, the five‐year period shall be tolled for the period of the concealment. For purposes of this subdivision, “fraudulent concealment” means the [person](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(u))knows of material facts related to their duties under this title and knowingly conceals them in performing or omitting to perform those duties, for the purpose of defrauding the public of information to which it is entitled under this title.

[**(c)**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.199.70(c)) If, upon being ordered by a superior court to produce any documents sought by a subpoena in any administrative proceeding under this title, the [person](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(u))alleged to have violated this title fails to produce documents in response to the order by the date ordered to comply therewith, the five‐year period shall be tolled for the period of the delay from the date of filing of the motion to compel until the date the documents are produced.

[**1798.199.75.**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.199.75)

[**(a)**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.199.75(a)) In addition to any other available remedies, the Agency may bring a civil action and obtain a judgment in superior court for the purpose of [collect](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(f))ing any unpaid administrative fines imposed pursuant to this title after exhaustion of judicial review of the Agency’s action. The action may be filed as a small claims, limited civil, or unlimited civil case, depending on the jurisdictional amount. The venue for this action shall be in the county where the administrative fines were imposed by the Agency. In order to obtain a judgment in a proceeding under this section, the Agency shall show, following the procedures and rules of evidence as applied in ordinary civil actions, all of the following:

[**(1)**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.199.75(a)(1)) That the administrative fines were imposed following the procedures set forth in this title and implementing regulations.

[**(2)**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.199.75(a)(2)) That the defendant or defendants in the action were notified, by actual or constructive notice, of the imposition of the administrative fines.

[**(3)**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.199.75(a)(3)) That a demand for payment has been made by the Agency and full payment has not been received.

[**(b)**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.199.75(b)) A civil action brought pursuant to subdivision (a) shall be commenced within four years after the date on which the administrative fines were imposed.

[**1798.199.80.**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.199.80)

[**(a)**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.199.80(a)) If the time for judicial review of a final Agency order or decision has lapsed, or if all means of judicial review of the order or decision have been exhausted, the Agency may apply to the clerk of the court for a judgment to [collect](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(f)) the administrative fines imposed by the order or decision, or the order as modified in accordance with a decision on judicial review.

[**(b)**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.199.80(b)) The application, which shall include a certified copy of the order or decision, or the order as modified in accordance with a decision on judicial review, and proof of [service](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(af)) of the order or decision, constitutes a sufficient showing to warrant issuance of the judgment to [collect](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(f)) the administrative fines. The clerk of the court shall enter the judgment immediately in conformity with the application.

[**(c)**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.199.80(c)) An application made pursuant to this section shall be made to the clerk of the superior court in the county where the administrative fines were imposed by the Agency.

[**(d)**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.199.80(d)) A judgment entered in accordance with this section has the same force and effect as, and is subject to all the provisions of law relating to, a judgment in a civil action and may be enforced in the same manner as any other judgment of the court in which it is entered.

[**(e)**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.199.80(e)) The Agency may bring an application pursuant to this section only within four years after the date on which all means of judicial review of the order or decision have been exhausted.

[**(f)**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.199.80(f)) The remedy available under this section is in addition to those available under any other law.

[**1798.199.85.**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.199.85)

Any decision of the Agency with respect to a complaint or administrative fine shall be subject to judicial review in an action brought by an interested party to the complaint or administrative fine and shall be subject to an abuse of discretion standard.

[**1798.199.90.**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.199.90)

[**(a)**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.199.90(a)) Any [business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d)), [service provider](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(ag)(1)), contractor, or other [person](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(u))that violates this title shall be subject to an injunction and liable for a civil penalty of not more than two thousand five hundred dollars ($2,500) for each violation or seven thousand five hundred dollars ($7,500) for each intentional violation and each violation involving the [personal information](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(v)) of minor [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i))s, as adjusted pursuant to paragraph (5) of subdivision (a) of Section [1798.185](https://thecpra.org/#1798.185), which shall be assessed and recovered in a civil action brought in the name of the people of the State of California by the Attorney General. The court may consider the good faith cooperation of the [business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d)), [service provider](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(ag)(1)), contractor, or other [person](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(u))in determining the amount of the civil penalty.

[**(b)**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.199.90(b)) Any civil penalty recovered by an action brought by the Attorney General for a violation of this title, and the proceeds of any settlement of any said action, shall be deposited in the [Consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i)) Privacy Fund.

[**(c)**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.199.90(c)) The Agency shall, upon request by the Attorney General, stay an administrative action or investigation under this title to permit the Attorney General to proceed with an investigation or civil action, and shall not pursue an administrative action or investigation, unless the Attorney General subsequently determines not to pursue an investigation or civil action. The Agency may not limit the authority of the Attorney General to enforce this title.

[**(d)**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.199.90(d)) No civil action may be filed by the Attorney General under this Section for any violation of this title after the Agency has issued a decision pursuant to Section [1798.199.85](https://thecpra.org/#1798.199.85) or an order pursuant to Section [1798.199.55](https://thecpra.org/#1798.199.55) against that [person](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(u))for the same violation.

[**(e)**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.199.90(e)) This section shall not affect the private right of action provided for in Section [1798.150](https://thecpra.org/#1798.150).

[**(c)**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.199.95(c)) The Attorney General shall provide staff support to the Agency until such time as the Agency has hired its own staff. The Attorney General shall be reimbursed by the Agency for these [services](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(af)).

[**1798.199.100.**](https://thecpra.org/#1798.199.100)

The Agency and any court, as applicable, shall consider the good faith cooperation of the [business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d)), [service provider](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(ag)(1)), contractor, or other [person](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(u))in determining the amount of any administrative fine or civil penalty for a violation of this title. A [business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d)) shall not be required by the Agency, a court, or otherwise to pay both an administrative fine and a civil penalty for the same violation.

[**SEC. 25. Amendment.**](https://thecpra.org/#SEC.%2025)

[**(a)**](https://thecpra.org/#SEC.%2025(a)) The provisions of this Act may be amended after its approval by the voters by a statute that is passed by a vote of a majority of the members of each house of the Legislature and signed by the Governor, provided that such amendments are consistent with and further the purpose and intent of this Act as set forth in Section 3, including amendments to the exemptions in Section [1798.145](https://thecpra.org/#1798.145) if the laws upon which the exemptions are based are amended to enhance privacy and are consistent with and further the purposes and intent of this Act and amendments to address a decision of a California state or federal court holding that a provision of the Act is unconstitutional or preempted by federal law, provided that any further amendments to legislation that addresses a court holding shall be subject to this subdivision.

[**(b)**](https://thecpra.org/#SEC.%2025(b)) Notwithstanding Section [1798.199.25](https://thecpra.org/#1798.199.25), the Legislature may authorize additional compensation for members of the California [Consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i)) Privacy Agency, if it determines that it is necessary to carry out the Agency’s functions, by a statute that is passed by a vote of a majority of the members of each house of the Legislature and signed by the Governor.

[**(c)**](https://thecpra.org/#SEC.%2025(c)) This section applies to all statutes amended or reenacted as part of this Act, and all provisions of such statutes, regardless of whether this Act makes any substantive change thereto.

[**(d)**](https://thecpra.org/#SEC.%2025(d)) The provisions of this Act shall prevail over any conflicting legislation enacted after January 1, 2020. Any amendments to this Act or any legislation that conflicts with any provision of this Act shall be null and void upon passage of this Act by the voters, regardless of the code in which it appears. Legislation shall be considered “conflicting” for purposes of this subdivision, unless the legislation is consistent with and furthers the purpose and intent of this Act as set forth in Section 3.

[**SEC. 26. Severability.**](https://thecpra.org/#SEC.%2026)

If any provision of this measure, or part of this measure, or the application of any provision or part to any [person](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(u))or circumstances, is for any reason held to be invalid, the remaining provisions, or applications of provisions, shall not be affected, but shall remain in full force and effect, and to this end the provisions of this measure are severable. If a court were to find in a final, unreviewable judgment that the exclusion of one or more entities or activities from the applicability of the Act renders the Act unconstitutional, those exceptions should be severed and the Act should be made applicable to the entities or activities formerly exempt from the Act. It is the intent of the voters that this Act would have been enacted regardless of whether any invalid provision had been included or any invalid application had been made.

[**SEC. 27. Conflicting Initiatives.**](https://thecpra.org/#SEC.%2027)

[**(a)**](https://thecpra.org/#SEC.%2027(a)) In the event that this measure and another measure addressing [consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i)) privacy shall appear on the same statewide ballot, the provisions of the other measure or measures shall be deemed to be in conflict with this measure. In the event that this measure receives a greater number of affirmative votes than a measure deemed to be in conflict with it, the provisions of this measure shall prevail in their entirety, and the other measure or measures shall be null and void.

[**(b)**](https://thecpra.org/#SEC.%2027(b)) If this measure is approved by the voters but superseded by law by any other conflicting measure approved by voters at the same election, and the conflicting ballot measure is later held invalid, this measure shall be self‐executing and given full force and effect.

[**SEC. 28. Standing.**](https://thecpra.org/#SEC.%2028)

Notwithstanding any other provision of law, if the State or any of its officials fail to defend the constitutionality of this Act, following its approval by the voters, any other government agency of this State shall have the authority to intervene in any court action challenging the constitutionality of this Act for the purpose of defending its constitutionality, whether such action is in state or federal trial court, on appeal, or on discretionary review by the Supreme Court of California and/or the Supreme Court of the United States. The reasonable fees and costs of defending the action shall be a charge on funds appropriated to the California Department of Justice, which shall be satisfied promptly.

[**SEC. 29. Construction.**](https://thecpra.org/#SEC.%2029)

This Act shall be liberally construed to effectuate its purposes.

[**SEC. 30. Savings Clause.**](https://thecpra.org/#SEC.%2030)

This Act is intended to supplement federal and state law, where permissible, but shall not apply where such application is preempted by, or in conflict with, federal law, or the California Constitution. The provisions of the Act relating to children under 16 years of age shall only apply to the extent not in conflict with Children’s Online Privacy Protection Act.

[**SEC. 31. Effective and Operative Dates.**](https://thecpra.org/#SEC.%2031)

[**(a)**](https://thecpra.org/#SEC.%2031(a)) This Act shall become effective as provided in subdivision (a) of section 10 of article II of the California Constitution. Except as provided in subdivision (b), this Act shall become operative January 1, 2023, and with the exception of the right of access, shall only apply to [personal information](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(v))[collected](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(f)) by a [business](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(d)) on or after January 1, 2022.

[**(b)**](https://thecpra.org/#SEC.%2031(b)) Subdivisions (m) and (n) of Section [1798.145](https://thecpra.org/#1798.145), Sections [1798.160](https://thecpra.org/#1798.160), [1798.185](https://thecpra.org/#1798.185), [1798.199.10](https://thecpra.org/#1798.199.10) through [1798.199.40](https://thecpra.org/#1798.199.40), and [1798.199.95](https://thecpra.org/#1798.199.95), shall become operative on the effective date of the Act.

[**(c)**](https://thecpra.org/#SEC.%2031(c)) The provisions of the California [Consumer](https://thecpra.org/#1798.140(i)) Privacy Act of 2018, amended or reenacted by this Act, shall remain in full force and effect and shall be enforceable until the same provisions of this Act become operative and enforceable.
